The direct answer

What is business process automation?

Business process automation uses software to execute or coordinate repeated work: collecting information, applying rules, moving data, assigning ownership, creating documents, notifying people, and recording what happened.

The strongest opportunities sit between tools or teams. A CRM may store the lead, a scheduling product may hold the appointment, and an accounting system may issue the invoice—but staff still move information and chase the handoffs. Integration and a focused workflow layer can remove that gap.

Automate the stable rules. Keep people in the decisions that need judgment, context, or accountability.

Build, buy, connect, or wait

Use the smallest responsible intervention.

PathChoose it whenWatch for
Configure a standard productThe important workflow is common and the product handles it with acceptable compromise.Paying for custom work that recreates mature commodity features.
Connect existing productsThe tools work individually, but the handoffs and shared reporting do not.API limits, data ownership, sync conflicts, and fragile one-off automations.

ROI needs a baseline

Record current staff time, throughput, rework, missed follow-up, and cycle time before implementation. A credible business case compares the same measures after launch and includes ongoing operating cost.

Engagement path
MapChooseShipMeasureCost guidance

Map the actual workflow

Identify triggers, owners, data, decisions, exceptions, existing tools, and the finished outcome. Screens and real examples are more useful than abstract requirements.

Choose the smallest intervention

Compare configuration, integration, and custom development. Define one release that removes a constraint without turning a process improvement into a platform rewrite.

Build, verify, and prepare the operation

Review working software throughout the build. Test permissions, failure states, data movement, and human fallback—not only the ideal path. Clarify hosting, code, credentials, documentation, and support ownership before release.

Measure the operational result

After launch, compare the baseline with actual adoption and outcomes. The next release should respond to evidence, not simply the backlog.

FAQs

Questions before automating a Bay Area workflow.

What processes are good candidates for automation? +

Repeated, rules-based workflows with clear inputs and outcomes—especially data copying, routine approvals, document assembly, status updates, reconciliation, and reporting.

Should we buy software or build it? +

Buy when a mature product handles the important workflow with acceptable tradeoffs. Connect products when the gap is in handoffs. Build when a repeated, valuable, business-specific gap remains.

Do you serve Marin and Napa? +

Yes. Both are included in Bay Area service coverage. 57 is based in Rohnert Park and does not claim separate Marin or Napa offices.

Can automation use AI? +

Yes, when the task, data boundaries, review requirements, and failure handling are explicit. Deterministic rules remain preferable when a wrong answer is costly and the decision does not need generative reasoning.

How do we estimate ROI? +

Record current time, rework, cycle time, missed follow-up, and delayed revenue. Compare those measures after launch and include build, hosting, support, and change costs.
