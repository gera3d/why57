# Partner and referral outreach templates

These are review-ready drafts, not permission to send. Replace every bracketed field, verify the destination, and remove any claim that is not supported by an approved source. Never describe someone as a partner, referrer, or endorser until they have agreed to that relationship.

## Shared positioning

**One sentence:** 57 helps teams turn manual operations and AI-built prototypes into owned, production-ready software.

**Good referral signals:** repeated spreadsheet handoffs, manual status updates, disconnected systems, a customer portal need, or a working AI-built prototype that now needs real users, security, integrations, and operational ownership.

**Low-friction next step:** a short fit conversation or the production-readiness checklist. Do not ask the recipient to “sell” 57.

## 1. Agency collaboration

**Subject:** A delivery backstop for custom product work

Hi [Name] — I’m reaching out because [specific agency work or shared context].

57 builds the operational software that often sits just beyond a marketing or design engagement: client portals, workflow automation, integrations, and production hardening for AI-built prototypes.

If a client asks for something outside your preferred delivery scope, we can evaluate it without crowding the relationship. We can work behind the scenes, collaborate visibly, or simply give you a clear “not a fit.” Your team stays the lead unless we agree otherwise.

Would a 20-minute capability swap be useful? I can bring two or three concrete referral signals and the boundaries we use to avoid channel conflict.

— [Sender]

**Follow-up:** Hi [Name] — closing the loop with a practical resource: [readiness-checklist URL]. It is useful when a client has an AI-built prototype but nobody has assessed ownership, data, reliability, or launch risk. No need to respond unless that gap appears in your work.

## 2. Managed service provider (MSP)

**Subject:** When an IT request is really a software workflow

Hi [Name] — MSPs often see the operational bottleneck before anyone else: a spreadsheet acting like a database, duplicate entry between systems, or a team asking IT to maintain a one-off app.

57 handles custom workflow software, integrations, dashboards, and production hardening. We do not replace managed IT; the useful handoff is when the problem needs product engineering rather than another SaaS configuration.

If helpful, I can share a one-page “refer / do not refer” guide and learn which requests you prefer to keep in-house. Would [two specific time options] work for a short introduction?

— [Sender]

**Follow-up:** Hi [Name] — one simple referral test: if the request changes a business workflow and will need an owner, permissions, integrations, and ongoing releases, it may be software rather than support. Here is a neutral readiness checklist you can use without introducing us: [readiness-checklist URL].

**Follow-up:** Hi [Name] — sharing one artifact that may be useful during diligence: [readiness-checklist URL]. It separates prototype signal from production risk and gives us a common language for “keep, harden, or replace.”

The session would help local business owners assess code ownership, customer data, reliability, support, and measurable launch outcomes. It would be practical and vendor-neutral, built around a take-home checklist rather than a sales presentation.

If this fits your programming, could we compare audience needs, format, and your education guidelines? I would provide the outline and claims for review before anything is promoted.

**Follow-up:** Hi [Name] — here is the draft attendee checklist for review: [readiness-checklist URL or attachment]. I have not scheduled or promoted the session; I’d first want to confirm it serves your members and follows your content policy.

## Referral handoff snippets

### Partner to prospect

> I know a software team that works on [specific problem]. I have not assessed whether they are the right fit, but I can introduce you if you want a second perspective. They can start with a short fit conversation and will tell you if the work belongs elsewhere.

### Why57 acknowledgement

> Thanks, [Partner]. [Prospect], good to meet you. I understand the immediate question is [one-sentence problem]. I’ll keep the first conversation focused on fit, risk, and the smallest useful next step. [Partner], let us know whether you want to stay copied or hand this off.

### Not-fit response

> Thanks for the context. Based on [reason], we are not the strongest fit for this work. The next useful category of help is [specialist/type of provider], and the key question to ask is [question]. I do not want to force a software engagement where it will not help.

## Pre-send review

- [ ] The recipient and relationship context are real and current.
- [ ] The message explains why this person—not a list—is receiving it.
- [ ] No partnership, endorsement, exclusivity, or client permission is implied.
- [ ] Claims and links match an approved source.
- [ ] A human has reviewed tone, recipient, and call to action.
- [ ] The link has the intended UTM values and resolves correctly.
- [ ] Opt-out or “no need to respond” language is appropriate to the channel.
